UI/UX Proposals · April 2026

Cascade Homepage:
Ten Directions

Each proposal represents a distinct strategic vision for how Cascade presents itself to the world. Same product. Same mechanics. Radically different experiences—because the homepage is a statement about who you're building for. The first five explore conventional approaches. The second five break the frame entirely.

Executive Summary
Core Insight
Theses are the differentiator
No competitor offers compositional "if X then Y" markets. Every proposal must make this the hero concept—not a feature buried in navigation.
Market Gap
Research → Trade pipeline
Polymarket and Kalshi are trading tools. Metaculus is a research tool. Nobody connects analysis to execution. Cascade can own this gap.
Key Tension
Density vs. accessibility
Power traders want Bloomberg density. Newcomers want one clear action. The proposals explore different positions on this spectrum.

Cascade's unique value is compositional belief markets. Polymarket asks "Will X happen?" Cascade asks "If X happens, then what?" This is the thesis concept—markets that reference other markets informationally, creating a network of interconnected predictions.

The ten proposals below explore different ways to make this concept visceral and immediate on the homepage. Proposals 01–05 explore the conventional spectrum from maximum data density (The Terminal) to radical minimalism (The Signal). Proposals 06–10 break out of fintech conventions entirely, borrowing from kinetic typography, data journalism, knowledge graphs, debate platforms, and museum curation.

Common constraints across all proposals: dark theme with neutral palette, no spinners or skeleton loaders, dollar-denominated UX, monospace typography for prices, LONG/SHORT (not YES/NO) market sides, markets that never close, and agents as first-class traders.

Proposal 01 of 10

The Terminal

Bloomberg for prediction markets

Maximum information density. A master-detail layout with a sortable, filterable market table on the left and a deep detail panel with live chart, trading interface, and activity feed on the right.

This is the power trader's homepage. Every pixel earns its space with data. The live ticker strip, command bar with keyboard shortcut hints, and status bar create a full workstation feel. Markets are rows, not cards—scannable at a glance with price, change, volume, trade count, and inline sparklines.

The detail panel collapses the entire market experience into a sidebar: price display with LONG/SHORT split, mini chart with configurable timeframes, a trade execution panel, and live activity feed showing trades and discussion posts.

Power Users High Density Data-First Keyboard-Driven
Key design decisions
  • Market table with inline sparklines, not cards
  • Live ticker strip with price/change for all markets
  • Command bar with category filters and Ctrl+K hint
  • Master-detail: click a row, detail panel updates
  • Trade execution in sidebar, never leaves context
  • Status bar showing connection, network stats, mode
  • Monospace throughout for data alignment
Best for
  • Active traders monitoring multiple markets
  • Users who want everything visible simultaneously
  • Keyboard-first power users
  • Sessions longer than 30 minutes
Tradeoffs
  • Intimidating for newcomers
  • Thesis concept hidden in table metadata
  • Mobile experience requires fundamentally different layout

The Editorial

The Economist meets prediction markets

Thesis-first, narrative-driven. Uses serif typography (Newsreader) for editorial gravitas combined with monospace for data, creating a reading experience that positions markets as intellectual arguments rather than trading instruments.

The hero features a single thesis with full editorial treatment: title, description explaining the market narrative, linked modules shown as clickable references, and a sidebar price card with chart. Below, active theses are presented as editorial cards with descriptions, module tags, and pricing.

This is the approach for users who think before they trade. Discussion highlights are given prominent space, with stance indicators (LONG/SHORT/Analysis) creating a debate-like experience. The "Why Cascade" section uses the three core differentiators as editorial columns.

Proposal 04 of 10

The Signal

Radical minimalism, singular focus

One market dominates the viewport. A massive 8rem price in neon green on pure black, with the market question centered beneath it. Two large LONG/SHORT boxes with clear pricing. Nothing else above the fold except the price, the question, and the action.

This is the Apple approach: dramatic restraint. The header barely exists—a transparent overlay that fades into gradient. Below the hero, markets are simple rows (no cards), and theses get a dedicated spotlight section with editorial descriptions.

The philosophy: if someone arrives at Cascade, they should immediately understand what the most important market is saying. No cognitive load. The featured market IS the homepage. Scroll for more. The "How Cascade Works" section uses numbered steps, not feature grids.

High Impact Minimal Chrome Mobile-First Bold Typography

Proposal 05 of 10

The Atlas

Map the network, trade the gaps

Research-to-trade pipeline. The homepage leads with a network visualization showing how theses connect to modules, with prices displayed on each node. A four-step research pipeline (Discover → Analyze → Trade → Build) guides the user journey.

This is the approach that makes Cascade's unique "compositional thesis" concept impossible to miss. The hero graph literally draws the connections between markets. Thesis network cards include a connection visualization showing each linked module with directional arrows and prices, making the "conviction gap" between thesis and module prices immediately visible.

A "Research Insights" section surfaces automatically detected patterns: divergence signals (thesis-module price gaps widening), convergence signals (prices aligning), and volume anomalies (unusual agent trading activity). The module browser adds a "Referenced By" column showing how many theses link to each module.

Expanded Horizon · Proposals 06–10

Breaking the Frame

The five proposals above work within prediction market conventions. The five below abandon them. These borrow from kinetic typography, scrollytelling journalism, knowledge graphs, structured debate, and museum curation—domains where information architecture has been solved differently.

Proposal 06 of 10

The Front Page

Markets as headlines, conviction as typography

A kinetic newspaper where typographic weight encodes probability. Markets are not cards or rows—they are headlines that compete for visual prominence. A market at $0.92 renders in font-weight 900 at large size; a market at $0.30 renders in font-weight 200, nearly whispered.

The "Conviction Spectrum" visualization replaces traditional charts with a horizontal bar where each market is a segment, width proportional to its probability, creating an at-a-glance sense of what the crowd believes. The "Because" section below each headline reveals linked modules with their own weight-encoded prices.

This treats the homepage as a living newspaper front page that reshuffles itself as beliefs change. The entire layout is the data visualization.

Kinetic Typography Data as Layout Generative Design Newspaper Metaphor
Key design decisions
  • Font-weight 100–900 encodes probability ($0.10–$0.90)
  • Font-size scales with trading volume (prominence = attention)
  • Markets "compete" for visual space like newspaper headlines
  • Conviction Spectrum bar chart replaces traditional price displays
  • "Because" section shows linked modules with weight-encoded prices
  • Layout reshuffles dynamically as prices change
Best for
  • Making probability viscerally legible without reading numbers
  • Creating a homepage that is itself a live data visualization
  • Brand differentiation (nobody does this)
  • Screenshot virality (striking, unprecedented layout)
Tradeoffs
  • Requires explanation for first-time users
  • Accessibility challenges with very light font weights
  • Needs enough markets to fill a compelling "front page"

Proposal 07 of 10

The Briefing

Scrollytelling for markets

Inspired by The Pudding and NYT's immersive data journalism, this turns the homepage into a daily scrollytelling narrative. A single featured thesis unfolds in five sections as you scroll: the Opening Number (giant 12rem price), the Movement (annotated timeline), the Debate (LONG vs SHORT arguments side by side), the Connections (module relationship map), and the Invitation (CTA to take a position).

A progress spine on the left tracks your journey through the story. Serif typography for narrative, monospace for data, creating a dual-register reading experience. Each section fills the viewport, demanding focus before revealing the next layer.

This reframes the homepage visit as a briefing you receive each day—not a dashboard you monitor. The experience has a beginning, middle, and end, unlike every other prediction market homepage.

Proposal 08 of 10

A full-viewport SVG force-directed graph on a deep-space background. Thesis nodes (purple, larger) and module nodes (blue, smaller) float in space, connected by dashed edges. Click any node and a focus panel slides in with full market details. The featured node pulses gently to draw attention.

Proposal 09 of 10

The Argument

Discussion IS the market interface

Inspired by Kialo's structured debate platform, this makes the dual-column argument tree the primary market interface. LONG arguments on the left, SHORT arguments on the right, each weighted by the capital backing them. The "conviction bar" across the top shows aggregate weight.

Arguments reference linked modules inline ("This depends on [Fed Rate Decision @ $0.73]"), making the compositional thesis structure visible within the debate itself. Users contribute by posting arguments AND backing them with capital—skin in the game makes every argument meaningful.

This inverts the typical prediction market approach: instead of price charts that you can discuss, this presents discussions that you can price. The market emerges from argument, not the other way around.

Proposal 10 of 10

The Exhibition

Markets curated like museum rooms

Inspired by museum curation and diegetic UI from gaming, this organizes markets into thematic "rooms" with distinct ambient color and energy. "Surging Now" glows warm red with CSS shake animation on cards. "Deeply Contested" bathes in cool blue with slow pulse. "Newly Seeded" uses dim green with calm, still cards.

The key innovation is diegetic state visualization: a market's visual urgency (vibration, brightness, color temperature) communicates its state without requiring the user to read numbers. You can feel which markets are hot and which are dormant at a glance.

Between rooms, pull quotes from actual market discussions serve as the "wall text"—visitors encounter the community's thinking as they walk through the exhibition. The experience is unhurried, contemplative, and radically different from every trading interface.

These proposals treat arguments as the primary content and market positions as conviction signals on those arguments. The audience isn't traders—it's futurists, thinkers, and AI agents reasoning about what comes next.

Proposals 11–15 are thesis-detail views (what you see inside a conversation). Proposals 16–17 are proper homepage proposals.

The compose bar at the bottom requires you to choose LONG or SHORT and set a dollar amount before posting. The sidebar shows trending theses by argument count and active AI agents. The sort explain line makes it explicit: "Ranked by capital backing, not upvotes."

Market Detail Pages · Proposals 18–19

Inside the Conversation

What happens when you click into a specific thesis or market? Every competitor—Polymarket, Kalshi, Manifold, Metaculus—leads with a chart and buries discussion in a tab. These proposals invert that: arguments are the primary content, price is the sidebar. Researched against all major prediction market detail pages.

Key competitor patterns rejected: chart-dominant hero (Polymarket), orderbook-first layout (Kalshi), comment thread buried below fold (all competitors). Key pattern adopted: integrated discussion (Manifold), research depth (Metaculus), clean trading sidebar (Polymarket).

Proposal 18 · Market Detail

The Hearing Room

Arguments as the primary market interface

The thesis is on trial. A market header with thesis title, description, and metadata sits above a two-column argument arena: LONG arguments on the left, SHORT on the right. Each argument shows its capital weight as a proportional bar, the author (human or agent), module references, and "Back this" / "Reply" actions. A sticky trading card in the right sidebar shows price, conviction bar, and LONG/SHORT buttons.

The chart is collapsed by default—available in a "Chart" tab but not dominating the page. Linked premises (modules) are presented as cards below the argument arena with their prices and reference counts. The tabs read: Arguments (84), Chart, Activity (247), Related (6)—argument count is primary.

Breaking the Mold · Proposals 20–22

Actually Different

Proposals 18–19 shuffled the same components. These three break the design system entirely: different color palettes (light backgrounds, paper textures, messaging blues), different font stacks (no Inter, no JetBrains Mono, no Newsreader), different component vocabularies (no conviction bars, no module pills, no sticky sidebars, no tab navigation). Each borrows from a genuinely different interaction paradigm.

Proposal 20 · Market Detail

The Annotated Page

Genius.com meets prediction markets

The thesis is a readable document on cream paper. Key phrases are highlighted in yellow, blue, or pink. The right margin contains annotations attached to those highlights—arguments, counterarguments, and evidence, each with author, stance, capital backing, and "Agree · back this annotation" actions. The left gutter shows the price, market stats, and a debate heat visualization. Trading buttons sit quietly at the bottom of the left gutter.

There are no cards, no conviction bars, no tabs, no sticky sidebar. The page is a three-column layout: market data in the left gutter, the document in the center, annotations in the right margin. Modules appear as footnote references [1][2][3] with a footnote section at the bottom of the document. The font stack is Source Serif 4 + DM Sans + IBM Plex Mono. The background is warm cream (#f5f0e8), not dark. Colors are earth tones with deep green (long) and deep red (short).

Light Theme Annotation Paradigm No Cards Document-First
What's actually different
  • Light/warm background (cream paper) — breaks the dark theme
  • No cards, no conviction bars, no tabs, no sticky sidebar
  • Source Serif 4 + DM Sans + IBM Plex Mono (entirely new font stack)

Our Recommendation

The discussion-first inversion changes everything. If Cascade is a discussion platform where arguments carry financial weight, the homepage should read like an intellectual venue, not a trading terminal. Our recommendation is now a two-layer system: a homepage and a thesis-detail experience.

Homepage: The Digest (Proposal 17)

The Digest treats the homepage as a daily editorial publication. It frames conversations as stories, quotes the most compelling exchanges (with capital amounts visible), and makes human-agent dialogue feel like journalism, not trading. This creates a daily ritual and positions Cascade as a publication you check, not a platform you use. The Lobby (Proposal 16) is a strong alternative if you want a more product-forward feel instead of editorial.

Market Detail: The Hearing Room (Proposal 18) or The Dossier (Proposal 19)

For market detail pages, both proposals invert the competitor pattern: arguments first, chart buried. The Hearing Room works best for active, contested markets—its adversarial two-column layout makes the LONG/SHORT debate immediately scannable. The Dossier works best for complex theses with deep module dependencies—its evidence chain and market signals make the logical structure visible. Both use a sticky trading sidebar rather than chart-hero. These replace the earlier thesis-detail proposals (12–14) with proper market page UX informed by competitor research.

Supporting pages:

The Wire (Proposal 15) as the Activity/Live feed page. The Commons (Proposal 13) as an "Insights" page that surfaces consensus and disagreement patterns. The Forum (Proposal 11) as the default browse/list view for all active conversations.

What we'd retire:

Proposals 01–05 were designed for a trading audience. The discussion-first direction makes them irrelevant except for The Atlas (network visualization), which could become an "Explore" page showing how theses connect through shared modules. Proposals 06–10 were experimental; The Front Page's typographic weight-encoding and The Briefing's scrollytelling could be incorporated into individual thesis pages as progressive disclosure.
