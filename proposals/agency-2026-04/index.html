Four Directions — Not Four Skins

If Cascade is a product for defending a thesis, what should the rest of the product look like?

You already have twenty-two landing-page explorations. This review goes further: four fully-formed product identities, each with its own navigation, typography, color treatment, and market creation flow — not just a front door. Three sit on the desktop spectrum (analyst desk, daily gazette, Tufte data-grid); the fourth re-asks the question from the phone-first angle the existing product has never answered. Every direction is buildable on top of daisyUI + Tailwind with the hard brand rules respected (neutrals, emerald/rose, no pills, no gradients, USD-only, no Nostr jargon), and each takes a clear position on the questions you haven't asked yet.

A / 01

The Desk

Research workflow · 3-pane · Command bar

Cascade as an analyst's workstation. A persistent left rail for navigation, a main reading pane, and a right rail that carries context — your positions, linked markets, live discussion. Density without noise. Keyboard-first, command-palette-driven. Market creation becomes a short authoring wizard rather than a single form. For the trader who thinks of Cascade as part of their workflow, not a site they visit.

B / 02

The Gazette

Editorial · Daily issue · Author-first

Cascade as a daily briefing. The homepage is a front page with a masthead, a dateline, a lead story, and a rail of briefs. Market pages read like long-form op-eds with a pull-out trading aside. Authorship is celebrated — the creator's argument sits above the chart. Creation is framed as pitching a story, not filing a form. This direction leans into Cascade's real edge: markets as arguments with a price attached. It pushes against one of the hard rules (serif display) — worth discussing.

C / 03

The Trace

Tufte · Data-first · Zero chrome

Cascade as pure information. No cards, no boxes, no headers — only the data. The homepage is a single dense grid of markets with sparklines. The market page is a chart with annotations. Discussion is footnoted, not central. Every trade is a dot. Every argument earns its real estate by being informative, not decorative. This is the direction that respects the existing style guide most literally and pushes the bar on density and restraint.

H / 04

The Study

Workspace chrome, essay content · Category-nav

Re-derived from first principles in response to "Gazette is too newspaper-y, Desk is too spreadsheet-y." The synthesis isn't a skin-swap: the product's chrome is a quiet workspace (persistent category rail, breadcrumbs, right-side context, command palette), but every claim gets editorial typography — the sentence gets air, the author's case reads like well-laid-out prose, the price pulls out as a measurement, not a sales pitch. Serif appears only on claim content, never on chrome. Categories (Geopolitics, Macro, Sports, AI, Tech, Culture, Policy) are nav destinations, not newspaper sections.

J / 05

The Floor

Twitter-chrome · heterogeneous feed · social graph

Three-column shell — bigger left nav with icons + labels, center feed, right rail — inheriting The Study's serif-on-claim rule but moving to a social-graph-first home. The feed is mixed: new claims from writers you follow, replies on markets in your watchlist, big trades, price alerts on claims you hold, hot threads. Tabs filter the stream (For You · Following · Replies · Markets · New claims · Big moves). Compose at top is the highest-leverage action: writing a new claim. The rail adds Notifications, Lists, Following — Twitter-shaped discovery without the noise.

K / 06

The Column

Substack-inspired · author-first · subscriber inbox

What if Cascade were not a feed of everything, but an inbox of writers you chose to hear from? Home is your subscriptions — new claims, case revisions, and updates to positions you hold, grouped by time (Today · This week · Earlier). The unit is the writer. Each inbox card has a publication tagline ("Corporate timing, weekly-ish"), an excerpt of the case, and the price as a measurement off to the side — never the foreground. Categories demote to a subscription toggle in the right rail. Chrome is serif-heavy and reading-shaped. Uses a warm ink accent (#efe7d3) for publication names only — still within the brand's neutral + emerald/rose palette.

G / 07

The Feed

Mobile-native · Hold-to-commit · Thumb-first

Cascade designed as a phone-first product, not a responsive afterthought. The homepage is a vertical scroll-snap feed — one claim fills the viewport, swipe up for the next. Back YES / Back NO are dock-bottom thumb buttons, and they require a deliberate hold, not a tap — the commitment matches the copy ("put money behind your view"). The create flow reuses the same gesture to publish, so authorship and trading feel like the same act. This direction answers the audience you're probably under-serving: the five-minute-between-meetings user forming conviction on a phone.

The unasked questions

What these proposals decide for you.

Every decision left unmade becomes an inconsistency later. Here's what each direction commits to that the current product doesn't.

Asked in your brief

  • > Typography system
  • > Color palette
  • > Layouts
  • > Navigation

Unasked — decided anyway

  • + Information density mode (compact/default/roomy)
  • + Empty-state voice and first-run feel
  • + Motion language (how a new trade arrives)
  • + Authorship surface (who owns a claim)
  • + Linked-market relationships in the UI
  • + Mobile treatment (compact vs. scroll)
  • + Creation as a form vs. as an authoring tool
  • + Trade confirmation voice and celebration budget
